Voicing question: natural quill vs Delrin plectra on a French double
Forum: Harpsichord Building & Maintenance • Posted by ClavecembaloFan • November 2001

I am in the process of voicing my Zuckermann French double kit (8', 8', 4' disposition). The kit comes with Delrin plastic plectra. A friend who plays professionally says I should use natural quill (crow or condor) for a more authentic baroque sound. Delrin seems easier to maintain but quill supposedly sounds better. Has anyone compared both directly? Is the tonal difference noticeable to a non-expert ear?

Having built harpsichords since 1973 with both materials, my honest assessment: the tonal difference exists but is subtle enough that most listeners (and many players) cannot distinguish them in a blind test. What IS different is the tactile and dynamic response. Natural quill has a more complex "break" and allows for a slightly wider dynamic range through voicing adjustments. Delrin, properly voiced, is indistinguishable to all but specialists. For a first kit build, I strongly recommend Delrin. Learn voicing on it first. Natural quill requires humidity control and regular maintenance that will frustrate a beginner.

I play both lute and harpsichord, and Hans is right on all counts. I'd add: condor quill (which you mentioned) is difficult to source legally in many countries (protected bird), and the quality of crow quill varies widely by individual bird and time of year. Professional harpsichordists who use quill spend considerable time selecting and preparing their plectra. For a kit build this overhead is counterproductive to learning the instrument.

Voicing Delrin: the plectrum should have a slight taper toward the tip (thinner at the point that contacts the string). The goal is a quick clean "snap" with no scratching noise on the upward return stroke. Use a very sharp craft knife or voicing tool. Work slowly - remove material in thin slices. It is easy to remove too much and make a weak plectrum; you cannot add material back. I recommend voicing one register at a time, testing each jack on the string before moving on.

Built the same kit two years ago. The voicing took me about three months of gradual adjustment. Don't rush it - live with the instrument for weeks between voicing sessions. Your ear improves and you'll hear things later that you couldn't hear when you started.
