My sourdough starter suddenly smells like nail polish remover - acetone/alcohol - is it dead?
Starter Troubleshooting • Posted by FirstLoaf_Meg • September 2001

My starter is 3 weeks old and was going well - bubbling every day, doubling reliably. Yesterday I forgot to feed it and it sat on the counter for 36 hours. Now it has a strong acetone/nail polish smell, liquid on top (I've heard this called "hooch"), and zero bubbles. The surface looks slightly pink/orange. Is my starter salvageable or do I need to start over? I'm heartbroken - I've named it.

WildYeast_Barbara — Master Baker — Posts: 8,221 — Sep 3, 2001

Meg, your starter is almost certainly NOT dead - it's hungry and stressed, but salvageable. Here's what happened:

Acetone/alcohol smell = the wild yeast ran out of sugars and switched to producing ethanol. This is normal starvation behavior, not death.

Hooch (liquid layer) = alcohol separation. Normal in an unfed starter. You can pour it off or stir it back in (stirring back adds a sharper flavor to bread).

The pink/orange color is the one thing to watch - this can indicate contamination (Serratia marcescens bacteria can cause pink/orange coloration). If it's pink, discard and start fresh. If it's more a rusty-tan color from oxidation, that's normal.

Feed it now: discard all but 50g, feed 50g flour + 50g water. Repeat every 12 hours for 2-3 days. It should recover its normal yeasty smell and bubble activity.
RyeBread_Gunnar — Posts: 3,445 — Sep 3, 2001

I'll add: the "hooch" layer smells like alcohol because it IS alcohol - ethyl alcohol produced by fermentation. It's not toxic, just a sign of neglect. Old-time bakers called the hooch layer "the starter's way of complaining." Your starter is complaining, not dying. The acetone note usually fades within one or two good feedings once the yeast colony rebounds.

WildYeast_Barbara — Posts: 8,221 — Sep 4, 2001

Yes! Adding a tablespoon of whole rye or whole wheat to a recovering starter is excellent. The outer bran layer carries more native wild yeast and bacteria than white flour. Even a 10% rye addition (10g rye to 90g AP) gives the colony more to work with. Return to all-purpose once it's reliably doubling again. Orange-tan at the edges is just oxidation - you're fine.
LevainLover_Cecile — Posts: 1,892 — Sep 5, 2001

Counter temperature matters enormously for recovery speed. If your kitchen is under 68°F the starter will be sluggish even when fed. Put it somewhere warm: top of the refrigerator (the motor creates gentle warmth), inside the oven with just the oven light on, or near (not on) a warm appliance. Ideal recovery temp is 75-80°F.
