Decanting a 1996 Barolo - how many hours and which decanter?
Italy • Piedmont • Posted by CellarMaster_Hugo • October 2003

Opening a bottle of 1996 Bartolo Mascarello Barolo this Saturday for a special dinner. Seven guests, all experienced wine drinkers. The wine has been stored correctly (55F, 70% humidity, on its side) since I purchased it at release. It has considerable sediment visible. I plan to stand it upright Thursday to let the sediment settle. Questions: (1) How long to decant before serving? (2) Traditional duck-neck decanter or should I use a wider Burgundy-style decanter? (3) Should I taste a small amount first to assess the condition of the wine?

Piemontese_Paolo — Italian wine specialist — Posts: 4,219 — Oct 6, 2003

Hugo, the 1996 Mascarello is one of the great bottles of the decade. Handle with care.

For decanting time: 1996 Barolo from Bartolo Mascarello is traditionally structured - high tannin, high acid, needing considerable air. I would decant 3-4 hours before serving. Some colleagues would say up to 6 hours. Do NOT use a wide Burgundy decanter - the surface area oxidizes the wine too aggressively and you risk losing the aromatics. Use the narrow duck-neck decanter: let the wine breathe naturally through the neck opening. Pour slowly down the inside wall.

Yes, taste immediately after decanting. At 7 years, it should still have structural tannins but should be opening up with violet, tar, and dried rose petal notes.
NebbiolaraLucia — Posts: 2,108 — Oct 6, 2003

Agreeing with Paolo on the duck-neck decanter. The 1996 vintage in Piedmont was extremely tannic across the zone and the Mascarello wines especially are built for very long aging. One addition: do the standing up on Wednesday evening rather than Thursday to give the sediment more time to fully consolidate at the bottom. After 7 years on its side, the sediment may be a paste - give it 48 hours upright.
CellarMaster_Hugo — OP — Oct 7, 2003

Both of you have confirmed what I hoped - the duck-neck, not the Burgundy. I have both and was second-guessing myself. Standing up moved to Wednesday evening. Lucia - how will I know if the wine has gone over? What are the signs that a 7-year Barolo is past its window even if stored well?
Piemontese_Paolo — Posts: 4,219 — Oct 7, 2003

Signs a Barolo has gone over: colour that has turned entirely tawny/brown at the centre (some browning at the rim is normal for age); a smell of vinegar or wet cardboard; tannins that have precipitated completely leaving only flat acidity. The 1996 Mascarello, correctly stored, should have 10-15+ more years of life. Seven years is still quite young for this wine. You should be fine - taste immediately after decanting and you'll know within 30 seconds.
Anonymous — Guest — Oct 8, 2003

Use a candle or a flashlight when you pour it from bottle to decanter, held below the neck. As the sediment reaches the neck you'll see it in the light and stop pouring. That's the traditional method and it works perfectly. Leave the last 1/4" of liquid in the bottle with the sediment.
NebbiolaraLucia — Posts: 2,108 — Oct 8, 2003

One more thought: serve at no more than 60-62F. Barolo served too warm loses structure and the alcohol becomes prominent and hot on the palate. If your dining room is warm, decant in a cooler room and bring to the table just before serving, not hours ahead.
CellarMaster_Hugo — OP — Oct 12, 2003

Report from Saturday: the wine was extraordinary. 4 hours in the duck-neck, candlelight pouring, 62F serving temperature as Lucia suggested. The color was deep ruby with just a hint of garnet at the rim. Aromas of dried rose, tar, leather, and something almost smoky. On the palate, still structured but every tannin was beautifully integrated. All seven guests agreed it was the finest bottle they had tasted in years. Salute, and thank you both.
