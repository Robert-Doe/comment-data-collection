1963 Rolex Submariner ref 5512 - four-liner dial, non-serif coronet - authentic?
Authentication • Rolex • Posted by NewToVintage_Carl • November 2002

Acquired this watch from an estate sale. The seller believes it's a 1963 Rolex Submariner ref 5512 based on the case serial (circa 1963). The dial is matte black, four-line text (OYSTER PERPETUAL / SUBMARINER / 200m=660ft / SWISS MADE), non-serif "Rolex" text, and a coronet that appears non-serif. The hands are original tropical (gilt) patina. Movement appears to be Cal 1560. I've done what research I can but this is my first vintage Rolex. Photos available on request. Is this a configuration that existed on genuine 5512s?

Carl, the configuration you describe is consistent with a genuine 1963 5512 transitional period dial. The four-line dial on the 5512 appeared from approximately 1959-1964. The non-serif coronet (sometimes called "non-serif crown") is correct for this period - the serif coronet appeared later on the 5513/1680. Cal 1560 is correct for 5512 in this era (before the switch to Cal 1570 circa 1965).

That said: the 5512 is a heavily faked watch. I would not reach a conclusion without photos. Post them to the authentication sub-forum. Key things we'd look for: crown guards profile, case finishing, lug holes, case back engravings, movement serial vs case serial alignment.

The "tropical" gilt hands (brown/chocolate patina on what were originally gilt) are a positive sign. Fakes rarely replicate the specific tropical patina correctly - it requires genuine age and specific storage conditions. Does the patina appear under magnification to have natural color gradation from center to edge? Fake "tropical" hands usually look uniformly painted. The 200m=660ft depth rating is correct for pre-1967 Submariners, which is consistent with your serial.

For vintage Rolex authentication in North America: both Tourneau and Govberg Jewelers in Philadelphia have in-house vintage specialists. There are also specialist auction houses (Antiquorum, Sotheby's Geneva sale) that do written authentication for pieces consigned to them. Most charge $100-200 for a letter of authentication. For a 5512 this is always worth it.

If authentic, the tropical gilt hands alone are a significant value driver. Many collectors specifically seek the tropical patina - it cannot be replicated on modern dials. Preserve them exactly as they are. Do not attempt cleaning or any conservation treatment.
