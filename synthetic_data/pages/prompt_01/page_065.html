Swivel knife keeps slipping off curved cuts - beveler lines not matching my traced pattern
Tooling Technique • Posted by FirstTooling_Marcus • May 2002

Working on my first floral leather carving on 8-9 oz veg-tan. I wet the leather and trace the pattern with a stylus. When I use the swivel knife to cut the design lines, the straight lines look okay but my curved cuts keep slipping outward, especially on tight curves. The beveler then gives uneven impressions because the cuts aren't centered properly. My Tandy swivel knife has been sharpened. Is this a pressure, angle, or technique issue?

Marcus, swivel knife slip on curves is almost always a moisture and angle issue together. Here's the full breakdown:

Moisture: Veg-tan must be cased (wet) to the right degree for carving. If it's too wet, the knife slides. If it's too dry, it drags and tears. The correct moisture: wet the back, let it dry until the surface returns to near-natural color but feels cool to the touch (like a damp basement). This is called "case dry." Most beginners over-wet their leather.

Knife angle: The swivel knife should be held nearly vertical (85-90 degrees to the surface) for tight curves. Leaning it creates a bevel on the cut wall, which makes the blade want to slide outward around curves. Grip higher on the barrel, let the yoke pivot freely, and pivot FROM THE SHOULDER not the wrist on long curves.

On the knife sharpness question: a Tandy swivel knife is a good starting knife but the factory edge is not sharpened to carving quality. You need a ceramic or leather strop loaded with jeweler's rouge or diamond compound. The blade should cut freely with almost no downward pressure - if you have to push down, it's still dull. A properly sharp swivel knife feels like it's almost falling through the leather on the cutting pass.

The Tandy knife is perfectly adequate for learning - keep it and learn to sharpen it properly. A craftsman who can't sharpen their tools will always struggle regardless of tool quality. Get a leather strop (or make one from scrap leather glued to a board) and charge it with Flexcut Gold compound. Strop 20-30 passes before every carving session. You'll feel the difference immediately.

The "shiny wet surface" confirms over-wetting. Let your next piece sit 20-30 minutes after wetting the back before you begin tracing.

On tight curves specifically: rotate the leather, not the knife. For a tight circular curve, keep the knife in the same grip orientation and turn the leather piece on your marble slab as you cut. The knife follows a consistent line while the leather provides the curve direction. Professional carvers rotate their work constantly - it looks strange to beginners but it's the correct technique for precise curved cuts.
