Extract IPA smells strongly of sulfur after 48hrs - is it ruined?
Beginner Brewing • Posted by HopHead_Danny • October 2002

First ever homebrew. Northern Brewer extract kit, US-05 yeast, OG was 1.058 (right on target). Pitched Saturday at 72F, now 48 hours later it's fermenting like crazy (bubbling every 2-3 seconds) but there's a really strong sulfur smell. Not like rotten eggs exactly, more like struck matches. The airlock water is yellow-ish. Did I ruin this batch? Should I dump it?

DO NOT DUMP IT. Sulfur is completely normal during active fermentation with many yeast strains, and US-05 can definitely produce sulfur especially at slightly warmer fermentation temps. The yellow airlock water is just blowoff - completely harmless. What you're smelling is CO2 carrying sulfur compounds produced by active yeast metabolism. It almost always blows off completely within a week. 72F is a bit warm for US-05 ideally (it prefers 59-68F) but won't ruin the beer.

Walt is right. Lager yeasts are notorious for this but ale yeasts can do it too, especially under stress (too warm, too cold, underpitching, overpitching). Given you have vigorous fermentation and a target OG, your yeast is very healthy - just gassy. Give it two more weeks before even thinking about dumping. The sulfur will be undetectable in the finished beer 99 times out of 100.

MasterBrewer_Walt
Posts: 8,221 		Oct 15, 2002 12:30pm
Yes, move to 64F - that's actually the sweet spot for US-05. It'll ferment more cleanly and produce fewer off-flavors. Never use bubble count to determine when fermentation is done - airlocks can bubble from temperature changes, CO2 coming out of solution, etc. The ONLY way to know fermentation is done is to take gravity readings 2-3 days apart. Same reading = done. For a 1.058 OG IPA, target FG around 1.010-1.014.
