Phalaenopsis losing bottom leaves one by one - yellowing from base - normal or disease?
Phalaenopsis • Posted by OrchidBeginner_Theresa • March 2002

I've had a Phal from a grocery store for about 8 months. It rebloomed once which was exciting. But now I'm losing the lowest leaf about every 5-6 weeks - it yellows from the base up and drops off. No mushy spots, no visible pests, the roots look silvery (I think healthy?). Is this natural shedding or crown rot starting? The plant currently has 5 leaves and just put out a new leaf bud at the top. Growing in a west window, watering when dry.

Theresa, what you're describing is completely normal! Phalaenopsis are monopodial growers - they grow from a single crown and shed older lower leaves as the plant matures and new leaves emerge from the top. As long as:

• The yellowing starts at the base of the leaf and progresses slowly (weeks)
• There is no mushiness or water-soaked appearance
• New growth is emerging from the crown
• Roots look silvery when dry (healthy, not dead-brown or mushy)

...this is entirely normal leaf cycling. The plant is replacing old leaves with new ones. A Phal will usually maintain 4-6 leaves. You're seeing healthy plant behavior.

To distinguish normal shedding from crown rot (which is serious): crown rot starts AT THE CENTER of the plant at the growing tip - you'll see softness, mushy brown tissue, or a foul smell at the crown. What you describe - yellowing at the BASE of the lowest leaf - is shedding from the bottom up, the exact opposite direction. You're fine.

"Silvery" roots when dry = plump, healthy roots with an intact velamen layer. Green roots when wet is also healthy. Brown/shriveled/mushy roots are the concern.

Absolutely do NOT redirect aerial roots into the pot. Phals are epiphytes - in nature they grow attached to tree bark, not in soil. Their roots need air circulation. Aerial roots reaching for light, hanging over the pot edge, or going sideways are completely natural. Forcing them into dense growing medium will kill them. Let them be free.

You mentioned a west window - that's good for Phals (they prefer indirect bright light, not direct sun which burns the leaves). One thing to watch: west windows in many climates get direct afternoon sun in summer which can scorch the leaves (yellowing with crispy edges, different from your current issue). A sheer curtain for the hottest hours keeps them happy through summer.
