My Echeveria Lola is turning purple and getting soft at the base - did I kill it?
Plant Health • Posted by DesertNewbie_Rosa • June 2002

I have an Echeveria "Lola" that I've had for about 6 months in a south window in Portland, OR. Last month I repotted it into a bigger ceramic pot (no drainage hole - I thought the rocks in the bottom would help). Now the outer leaves are turning purple/translucent, the base of the stem feels soft and slightly mushy when I squeeze it, and it's starting to lean. The top rosette still looks okay. Is this root rot? Can it be saved? I'm really attached to this plant.

Rosa, the symptoms you describe are classic root rot, and the pot without drainage is almost certainly the cause. The "rocks in the bottom" method unfortunately does not work - it actually raises the water table inside the pot, creating a saturated zone right where the roots sit.

Immediate action: remove it from that pot right now. If the rosette top is still firm, the plant can be saved by a technique called "beheading" - here's how:

1. Use clean scissors and cut the stem just above where the rot begins (usually 1-2 inches up from the base). The cut end should be white/green, not brown or mushy.
2. Let the cutting dry, exposed to air, for 2-5 days until the cut end calloused over.
3. Place on (not buried in) well-draining succulent mix. It will grow roots in 2-4 weeks.

The original root base is likely unsalvageable if mushy throughout.

Dawn is exactly right about the rocks myth. It's one of the most persistent bad pieces of gardening advice ever printed. The solution to bad drainage is always drainage holes, never a drainage "layer." Any pot you use for succulents should have at least one good drainage hole. Portland's overcast light also makes Echeveria prone to etiolation and rot - is it getting direct sun, or just bright indirect?

Don't throw away the healthy leaves you removed! After rescuing the rosette, lay any plump firm leaves flat on dry succulent mix. Many will produce tiny plantlets ("pups") from the base in 4-8 weeks. It's the most satisfying thing in succulent keeping. Free plants from a tragedy is a good outcome.
