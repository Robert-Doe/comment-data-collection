Third year growing tomatoes in 5-gallon buckets - flowers but no fruit
Vegetables • Tomatoes • Posted by PatioPete • July 2001

I have a south-facing balcony on the 5th floor and have tried growing tomatoes in 5-gallon black plastic buckets three summers in a row. Each year the plants get enormous and flower profusely, but I get almost no fruit set. Maybe 5-6 tomatoes total per plant. I'm using MiracleGro Tomato fertilizer weekly. The plants look completely healthy. Am I doing something fundamentally wrong?

MasterGardener_Beth — Extension Master Gardener — Posts: 3,219 — Jul 8, 2001

Pete - "enormous plants, lots of flowers, no fruit" is one of the most common container tomato problems and has a specific cause: over-fertilization with nitrogen.

MiracleGro Tomato at the full rate weekly is almost certainly pushing your plants into pure vegetative growth (leaves, stems) at the expense of fruit set. The NPK on that fertilizer is 18-18-21 - the nitrogen is promoting leaf growth.

For fruiting: STOP feeding for 2 weeks, then switch to a lower-nitrogen fertilizer (look for something like 5-10-10 or similar) and feed ONCE every 2 weeks rather than weekly. Also: are you on the 5th floor? Wind can prevent pollination. See below.
UrbanFarmer_Carlos — Posts: 1,445 — Jul 8, 2001

Adding to Beth's excellent diagnosis: the 5th floor wind issue is real. Tomatoes are self-pollinating but the pollen transfer requires either wind vibration or bee visits. At height, bees may not visit regularly and the wind may blow the pollen away rather than vibrating the flowers. Solution: every morning, gently shake each flower cluster or use an electric toothbrush held against the flower stem to simulate bee vibration. Sounds odd but it works extremely well.

MasterGardener_Beth — Posts: 3,219 — Jul 9, 2001

For container tomatoes specifically: I use either Tomato-tone (Espoma brand, organic) or plain tomassol liquid feed. Both have lower N, higher P and K ratios which encourage fruiting. Also consider adding epsom salt (1 tablespoon per gallon, every 2 weeks) - the magnesium improves calcium uptake and reduces blossom end rot which is another common container problem.

Also: black buckets in full sun on a south-facing balcony will get EXTREMELY hot. Soil temperatures over 95F can prevent fruit set even when everything else is right. Consider wrapping your buckets in white fabric or painting them white to reflect heat.
HeatWatch_Gina — Posts: 789 — Jul 10, 2001

The black bucket heat point cannot be overstated. I measured my black container soil at 108F on a 90F day. That's way above the fruit-set threshold. I switched to white nursery containers and my tomato yields tripled that season with no other changes.
Anonymous — Guest — Jul 11, 2001

Also try growing a cherry tomato variety rather than a large beefsteak type in containers. Large varieties like Big Boy or Beefmaster need significant soil volume (10 gallons minimum) to produce well. Cherry tomatoes (Sweet 100, Sungold, Black Cherry) produce prolifically in 5-gallon containers and are almost impossible to stop fruiting once they get started.
