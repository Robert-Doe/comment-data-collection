Repotted Shimpaku juniper in October - branches now browning and dying back
Juniper Care • Posted by NewBonsai_Vincent • November 2001

I repotted my Shimpaku juniper about 3 weeks ago (early October). I had never repotted it since purchasing 2 years ago and the roots were very tight in the pot. The inner branches have started turning brown and dying back in sections - maybe 30% of the tree. The outer foliage still looks green. I'm in zone 6b (Ohio). I did trim some roots during repotting. Was October wrong timing? Can the tree recover?
  Bonsai Replies (9)
Sensei_Masahiro — Bonsai for 30 years — Posts: 10,224 — Nov 4, 2001

Vincent, October is indeed poor timing for repotting Shimpaku juniper in zone 6b. The preferred window is early to mid-spring, just before or as buds begin to swell - this gives the tree the entire growing season to regenerate new root tips and establish in the new soil.

Repotting in October gives the tree no recovery time before winter dormancy. Root-pruned trees need vigorous growth to rebuild their root system, and that growth stops when temperatures drop.

What you're seeing: Inner branch dieback is the tree consolidating its resources - reducing foliage load to match the reduced root capacity. This is a stress response, not necessarily terminal. The fact that outer foliage remains green is actually encouraging - the tree is prioritizing its most photosynthetically productive outer pads.
JuniperJim_Portland — Posts: 4,556 — Nov 4, 2001

Masahiro is right. The key now is winter protection. Do NOT leave this tree outside unprotected in zone 6b after a fall repot - the reduced root system cannot sustain the tree through a hard freeze. Options: unheated garage (below 40°F but above 10°F), cold frame, or a cool basement with minimal supplemental light. Junipers need cold dormancy but the root ball must not freeze solid.

Sensei_Masahiro — Posts: 10,224 — Nov 5, 2001

Leave dead branches until spring. Removing them now causes no benefit and creates open wounds during the vulnerable period. In spring, when you see new growth beginning, THEN assess and remove dead wood cleanly with sharp shears.

Over winter: water sparingly (the tree has reduced metabolic needs in cold dormancy) but do not let the root ball dry out completely. Check monthly. Do not fertilize until new growth in spring. Do not wire or style - any stress reduces chances of recovery. Simply let the tree rest.
ShohinSociety_Petra — Posts: 2,778 — Nov 6, 2001

Adding a spring recovery protocol for when you bring it out: start with gentle, diluted fertilizer (half-strength) as soon as you see new growth. The tree needs help rebuilding its root system. Full-strength fertilizer immediately after winter stress can damage tender new roots. I use a biweekly half-dose regimen for the first 6 weeks after a stressed tree wakes up.
Anonymous — Guest — Nov 8, 2001

Shimpaku are among the most resilient junipers in the bonsai tradition. Yamadori (collected wild) Shimpaku are sometimes found nearly dead and recovered over years of patient care. A stressed but not terminally damaged specimen like yours, protected properly, has a very good chance of healthy spring emergence. Don't give up on it.

SPRING UPDATE: Tree survived the winter in the garage with monthly watering as Sensei directed. When I brought it out in mid-March I was afraid - about half the interior branches were dead. But in April, the living outer pads began pushing new growth, and two new small branch buds appeared lower on the trunk where I had hoped for branching anyway. The dead interior is being removed this week.

Tree is alive, growing, and I believe will be healthier for having been repotted (though I wish I had done it in spring). Half-strength fertilizer started per Petra's advice. Thank you Masahiro, Jim, Petra, and the anonymous encouragement. The patience this community teaches is as valuable as any technical knowledge.
