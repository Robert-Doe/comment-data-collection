My players keep splitting the party no matter what I do - DM advice needed
DM Advice • Posted by FirstTimeDM_Riley • January 2003

Running my first campaign (D&D 3.5, 4 players, 3rd level). Every session the party splits into 2-3 sub-groups and goes off in different directions. Last session: the Rogue decided to sneak into the guild hall alone while the Fighter/Cleric wanted to talk to the guards and the Wizard decided to visit the library. I was running 3 simultaneous scenes for 4 hours. Two of the players sat bored for most of the session. The players say they're having fun but I'm exhausted and clearly the others aren't. Is there a way to handle this, or is it a player skill issue?

This is one of the most common first-time DM challenges, Riley. First: it's not a player skill issue, it's a table management issue and the solution is yours to implement.

Practical tools:
1) Establish a "time unit" system. Each sub-scene takes X minutes of in-game time. All scenes progress simultaneously. After each real-time scene, switch. Keep each sub-scene to 5-10 minutes max before cutting away.
2) Put pressure on the separated characters via shared objectives. If the Rogue hears something important that requires the Fighter to know, both have a reason to reunite.
3) Create a "party objective" that makes splitting genuinely costly rather than just inconvenient.

From a rules perspective: you're not obligated to run simultaneous scenes. You can simply say "We'll play this scene out, then we'll come back to where Rogue is." Give the bored players something to do during other scenes - sketch the floor plan of the building, decide what their character is thinking/planning, take notes on what was just said. Spectating can be rich storytelling too. The problem isn't the splitting, it's the boredom - solve the boredom.

Yes, say it explicitly! "We'll spend 10 minutes on the guild hall scene then cut to the library." Players adapt to the rhythm quickly. The "cut" is a storytelling device films have used for a century. Your players are adults who watch movies - they understand scene cuts.

The character notes idea from Greg is great and actually great roleplay - it's exactly what characters would be doing while waiting. Just make sure the Fighter doesn't accidentally learn things the Rogue's player was surprised to discover.

A longer-term solution: talk to your players OUT OF GAME about the pace problem. Don't make it "no splitting allowed" - that kills agency. Instead say "Hey, I'm finding it really hard to run 3 simultaneous scenes well. Can we agree that splits of 15-20 minutes per sub-scene maximum are the target?" Players who want to split will still split, but they'll converge faster. Most players don't realize how splitting impacts the DM AND the other players.

One in-game tool: have NPCs that serve as a social glue, asking the party questions that require the whole group's input. A guild master who says "I need ALL of you to agree before I'll give you this contract" forces the party to converge. Organic and non-railroady.
