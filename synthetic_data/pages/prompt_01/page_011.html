Ender's Game by Orson Scott Card
Review by BookwormPatricia • Reviewed: November 2000

Card's classic remains as powerful today as when it was published in 1985. The moral complexity of Ender's situation - trained as a weapon without his knowledge - raises questions about childhood, violence, and responsibility that never feel easy or resolved. Essential reading.

Rating: 5 out of 5 stars

Read this in one sitting on a Saturday when I was about fourteen and it permanently shaped how I read science fiction. The twist at the end still hits me on re-reads even knowing it's coming. The ethics of Ender's situation become more troubling the older you get, not less.
LiteraryLila • Nov 15, 2000
I'll be the dissenting voice. The prose is functional but not memorable. Card's ideas are excellent but he's not a stylist. Compare it to Gene Wolfe or Le Guin and the gap is obvious. That said the ideas are good enough to carry it. 4 stars from me.
BookwormPatricia • Nov 15, 2000 • [reviewer]
LiteraryLila - fair point about the prose. I think Card's strength is plotting and moral scenario construction rather than sentence-level craft. But for science fiction those are often more important. Le Guin comparison is interesting though - have you read "The Word for World is Forest"? Might be worth trying if you like SF that thinks seriously about violence and its costs.
HardSciFan • Nov 17, 2000
Ender's Game is fine but Speaker for the Dead is where Card really finds his voice. It's a completely different book, slower and more philosophical, but the questions it asks are more profound. Ender is a different character entirely. Read both together if you haven't.
