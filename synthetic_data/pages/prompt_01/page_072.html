Dynaco ST-70 left channel about 6dB low after full recap - bias okay, what else to check?
Dynaco • Posted by VintageAudio_Pete • March 2001

Just completed a full recap on my Dynaco ST-70 (original PCB, not a modified unit). Replaced all electrolytic caps with Nichicon and Sprague equivalents. Bias is set correctly on both channels (per the service manual). But the left channel is noticeably quieter - about 6dB by measurement - than the right. Both channels work and sound clean, just imbalanced. I've swapped tubes between channels and the imbalance stays with the channel, not the tubes. What should I check next?

Pete, tube swap confirming the fault stays with the channel is valuable diagnostic information. It rules out output tubes and points to the small-signal section or a passive component. On an ST-70 with a channel 6dB low after recap, check in this order:

1) Signal coupling capacitors: The ST-70 uses a coupling cap (originally 0.1uF) between the driver stage and each output tube grid. A wrong-value cap installed during recap will attenuate the signal. Verify both coupling caps are correct value and not accidentally swapped with different values from your parts kit.

2) Phase inverter resistors: The split-load phase inverter in the ST-70 uses a balanced pair of resistors. If one was disturbed or an incorrect value substituted during recap work, channel imbalance results.

3) Input sensitivity pot: The ST-70 has individual channel level trimmers. Check that these are set symmetrically.

Harold's list is exactly right. I'll add one more common ST-70 recap error: the cathode bypass cap on the driver tube (EL34/KT88 driver). On the original PCB there's a 25uF or 22uF cap on the V3 driver cathode. If this cap was installed in the wrong polarity or with an incorrect value, the driver stage gain changes. A 6dB channel difference is consistent with a driver stage gain error. It's worth measuring the cathode voltage on each driver tube - they should be equal within a few percent.

9.4V on the left driver cathode is definitely wrong - that's pointing directly at a cathode resistor issue, not just the bypass cap. The cathode bypass cap doesn't set the DC voltage; the cathode resistor does. Check the physical cathode resistor on the left channel V3 position - look for a damaged resistor, a cold solder joint, or (less likely) an accidentally wrong value. The bypass cap value affects frequency response and gain but not DC operating point. Your voltage difference suggests a passive component issue at the cathode resistor, not the cap.

When working near the ST-70's cathode resistors during a recap, it's easy to accidentally disturb solder joints that were already marginal (old Dynacos often have cold joints from the original assembly). The voltage you measured suggests a partial connection - perhaps a marginal joint that's conducting but at high resistance. In-circuit resistance measurement won't tell you much; you need to physically inspect and reflow the solder joints around that cathode resistor position.

Safety reminder: even with the ST-70 powered off, the filter capacitors hold lethal voltages (400V+) for many minutes after shutdown. Always discharge the caps through a series resistor before probing around the power supply section, and never touch the B+ rail nodes without confirming zero volts. High-voltage tube work requires respect for the charge storage.
