Black walnut hull dye - do I need a mordant?
Posted by WoolStudio_Heather • October 2001

I collected and dried black walnut hulls this autumn and want to dye some handspun wool yarn. Everything I've read says walnut is "substantive" and doesn't need a mordant. But some sources say alum makes the color brighter. Does anyone have experience with mordanted vs unmordanted walnut? What's the color difference and is it worth the extra step?
  Workshop Replies (9)
PlantDye_Margaret — Posts: 2,456 — Oct 8, 2001

Walnut is indeed substantive - the juglone binds directly to protein fibres (wool, silk) without a mordant. You will get a deep warm brown that is actually quite lightfast and washfast without any mordant. Alum pre-mordant shifts the color slightly greener and cooler, and some say brighter, but walnut already has such a strong personality that the difference is subtle. I'd start without mordant and see if you like the natural color first.
VermontSheepFarm — Posts: 788 — Oct 9, 2001

I've done both extensively. Unmordanted: deep chocolate brown. Alum mordanted: similar but with a slight olive cast. Iron afterbath (iron mordant): shifts to near-black, very dramatic. Copper mordant: greenish brown. All are beautiful. The walnut is forgiving - hard to ruin. Warning though: the dye bath will stain your hands, arms, pots, everything. Use old equipment and wear rubber gloves.

VermontSheepFarm — Posts: 788 — Oct 10, 2001

Yes, iron as a "saddle" or modifier at the end is fine without pre-mordanting. I use ferrous sulfate (available from dye suppliers) dissolved in warm water. Rusty nails work but the results are less controllable. Dissolve about 1/4 tsp ferrous sulfate per gallon of water, add your fiber for 15 minutes, rinse well. Be sparing - over-ironing weakens wool fibre ("saddening").
PlantDye_Margaret — Posts: 2,456 — Oct 10, 2001

Also worth knowing: you can use the same walnut hull bath multiple times. Each successive bath gives lighter values - useful for creating a graduated color palette from a single dye batch. Many dyers exhaust the bath completely rather than pouring it out. The last bath will give a pale tan.
SpinningWheelSue — Posts: 1,102 — Oct 12, 2001

One more tip: soak your hulls for several weeks (or months!) before dyeing if possible. The longer the soak the deeper the color. I collect hulls in October and don't dye with them until February. The water turns almost black - that's all usable dye. Strain the hulls out and you have a concentrated dye bath ready to use.
Anonymous — Guest — Oct 15, 2001

Just want to add: walnut also dyes cotton but the uptake is much less. Cotton needs a tannin pre-treatment (gallnuts, sumac, or oak galls) before walnut will really take. For wool you can ignore this entirely.
