1967 Mustang Fastback - complete floor pan replacement - where to start?
Mustang • Body & Rust Repair • Posted by MustangMike_TX • March 2003

Bought a 1967 Mustang Fastback project last month - body is very solid except the floors are completely gone (quarter panels good, trunk floor good, firewall solid). I need to do full floor pans front to back. I've done basic body work but never a full floor replacement. Should I do frame-off or leave the body on the frame? What panels, and in what order? AMD vs. NPD vs. Scott Drake? Budget is there for quality.

Leave the body on the frame for a floor job unless you have a rotisserie and a reason to go frame-off. Floor pan replacement on a unibody Mustang is straightforward with body on. Sequence matters though: do the torque boxes first (they're structural), then the inner rockers if they're compromised, then front floor pans, then rear seat floor. Never do one side at a time and leave the body unsupported - jack stands under the pinch welds at all four corners before you cut anything.
MustangMaven_Donna

For panels: AMD (Auto Metal Direct) is widely considered the best quality for 65-70 Mustangs - thicker gauge, better stampings, less fitting work. NPD is also excellent especially for trunk pans. Avoid Scott Drake floor pans - they're too thin and the stamping details are vague. For a fastback specifically, the rear seat floor and the transmission tunnel area are the tricky pieces. AMD sells a complete front-to-back floor kit for the fastback body style.

MustangMaven_Donna

The fit complaints are mostly from early 2001-2002 AMD production. Their 2003 tooling is much better - I did a rear seat floor on a 67 FB in January and it fit within 1/8" with minimal adjustment. Still plan to spend time fitting before welding - that's true of any aftermarket panel. The torque boxes: 67 fastback and coupe ARE the same, BUT the 67 uses different torque boxes than the 65-66. Make sure AMD part# prefix is "CE" not "CB" on your order.

Tools you'll need that people forget: flap disc grinder for prep, 3" cut-off wheel for old spot welds (don't use a plasma cutter - too much heat warping), spot weld drill bits (Blair or Rota-Broach), and a GOOD MIG welder (minimum 140A). Your welder matters more than which brand panel you buy. Weak welds on a unibody structure are a safety issue, not just an aesthetic one.

What Wes said on the welder. Also: seam seal every lap joint before putting carpet back in. Moisture infiltration through unsealed seams is how floors rust in the first place. Use 3M brushable seam sealer on all overlapping seams front AND back of the panel. One coat, 48hr dry. Don't skip this even if it means an extra day.

Before you order any panels, take exact measurements of your floor opening front-rear and side-to-side and compare to AMD's specs. Some 67s were slightly different from the factory and aftermarket panels may need trimming. 10 minutes with a tape measure now saves 3 hours of grinding later.
