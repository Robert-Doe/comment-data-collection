How did Harold die at Hastings? Arrow interpretation vs. sword - what do contemporary sources actually say?
Norman Conquest • Posted by HistoryUndergrad_Paul • March 2001

The popular image is Harold shot through the eye with an arrow - it's in every school textbook and derives from the Bayeux Tapestry. But my professor says this is contested and that some historians argue he was cut down by Norman knights. What do the actual primary sources say, and which position do historians currently favor? I'm writing a third-year essay and want to engage with the scholarly debate accurately.

Medievalist_Prof_Crane — DPhil, Medieval History — Posts: 11,220 — Mar 7, 2001
Paul, this is a genuinely contested question that turns on the problem of source interpretation. The key primary sources are:

Bayeux Tapestry (c.1070s-1080s): The famous scene shows a figure labeled "HAROLD REX" with an arrow near his eye AND a falling figure being cut down by a knight. Historians disagree on which figure is Harold's actual death. Early scholars assumed the arrow figure; David Bernstein and others argue the cut-down figure is the death scene.

William of Poitiers (c.1070s): Claims Harold was killed by Norman knights, body mutilated. No mention of an arrow.

Amatus of Montecassino (c.1080s): Arrow to the face - but written in southern Italy, distant from events.

Carmen de Hastingae Proelio: If genuine (contested), describes a quadruple-knight killing.

The "arrow in the eye" became canonical from c.12th century texts and later received enormous reinforcement from the Tapestry's iconic image. Current scholarly consensus leans toward agnosticism - both accounts may contain partial truth, or Harold may have been wounded by arrow and then finished by knights.
NormanStudies_Yvette — Posts: 4,882 — Mar 7, 2001
Crane's summary is excellent. I would add: the Tapestry interpretation is complicated by later restorations. The 19th-century restoration work altered some stitching in the Harold death sequence, raising questions about whether the original arrows were actually pointing at the figure's eye or were near/above him. Scholarly photographs of the original weave are discussed in R.H.C. Davis's appendix to the 1985 catalogue.

For your essay: the most useful recent work is probably Stephen Morillo's "The Battle of Hastings" (1996) which collects and evaluates all sources, and David Howarth's "1066: The Year of the Conquest" for accessible narrative. Engage with at least one primary source directly - even in translation.

Medievalist_Prof_Crane — Posts: 11,220 — Mar 8, 2001
The Carmen controversy is instructive for your essay as a methodological case study. L.J. Engels argued in 1967 it was a 12th century forgery; Morton and Muntz (1972) defended its authenticity as a near-contemporary account by Guy of Amiens. The debate has not been resolved definitively. Most historians now use it cautiously as a source requiring corroboration.

Your instinct is correct: the "arrow in the eye" is a received tradition that hardened into apparent fact through repetition. This happens frequently in medieval historiography. Your essay could make the source-criticism process itself a theme - demonstrating that you understand how historical "facts" are constructed from contested evidence.
ConquestChronicles_Tom — Posts: 2,334 — Mar 9, 2001
One practical point for the essay: the Tapestry's scene shows both scenarios (arrow figure AND cut-down figure) in the same panel. Medieval narrative art often depicted sequential events in a single frame - so it's possible the Tapestry intends to show Harold first wounded by arrow, then killed by knights. This composite reading was argued by Bernstein (1986) and is now probably the majority view among Tapestry specialists.

For accessing primary sources in translation: the English Historical Documents volume II (ed. Douglas and Greenaway) contains William of Poitiers and other Norman sources in accessible translation. Most university libraries have it. Reading a passage of William of Poitiers directly and quoting from primary source is exactly the kind of engagement that distinguishes good third-year essays from summaries of secondary literature.
Anonymous — Guest — Mar 11, 2001
As a minor addition: there are also physical considerations. An arrow through the eye socket was a survivable battlefield wound (there are documented medieval cases). Some historians have therefore argued Harold was incapacitated by an arrow, then killed by knights - which would explain why both accounts contain truth. Battlefield deaths were rarely clean single-cause events.
