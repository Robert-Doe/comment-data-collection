Italic hand - nib keeps catching on upstrokes, tearing paper
Italic Script • Posted by NewNib_Harriet • March 2002

I've been practicing italic hand for four months with a Brause 2.5mm nib and Zaner-Bloser ink on Clairefontaine writing paper. My downstrokes are smooth but on upstrokes the nib catches and sometimes tears. I've tried adjusting my hand angle but it doesn't help. My pen angle is about 45 degrees as instructed. Is this a nib problem, paper problem, or technique problem?

MasterScribe_Anne — 30 years — Posts: 5,114 — Mar 3, 2002

Harriet, catching on upstrokes is almost always a nib orientation issue, not paper or ink. In italic hand, on upstrokes the nib travels against the direction it was designed for - the tines naturally dig in unless you lift the pen very slightly, reducing pressure to almost nothing.

The technique: upstrokes should use barely any pressure - the weight of the pen holder alone, no added finger pressure. Practice slow upstrokes in the air first to feel the difference. Also check that your nib tines are even - hold it up to a lamp. If one tine is lower than the other, the nib is sprung and will always catch. A new Brause 2.5 at £1.20 is cheaper than frustration.
ItalicInkGuild_Franz — Posts: 2,341 — Mar 3, 2002

Also: the pen angle matters enormously for upstrokes. Try rotating the pen barrel slightly so the nib edge is more parallel to your upstroke direction. In italic, a common error is keeping the full 45° nib angle even on thin upstrokes where the nib should be nearly edge-on. Watch Edward Johnston's description of "flattening" on upstrokes.

MasterScribe_Anne — Posts: 5,114 — Mar 4, 2002

Dip frequency: dip when the ink flow starts to thin, not when it runs out. An over-inked nib blobs at the start of a stroke; an under-inked nib scratches at the end. The ideal is consistent flow throughout each letter. With Brause nibs and Zaner-Bloser, every 4-6 letters typically. Touch the nib tip gently on the ink bottle rim to remove excess before writing.
VellumViolet — Posts: 778 — Mar 5, 2002

Clairefontaine is excellent paper generally but some batches have a slightly rough finish that grabs nibs. Try Rhodia or HP Premium 32lb inkjet paper (counterintuitively excellent for dip pens). If the catching completely disappears on different paper, you have your answer. Paper matters more than most beginners expect.
Anonymous — Guest — Mar 6, 2002

One additional tip: prepare new nibs before use. Factory nibs have a protective lacquer coating. Run a new nib through a flame briefly (1-2 seconds) or rub it with a pencil eraser, then rinse. This removes the coating and allows ink to flow properly from the first dip.
