When is it correct to burn an S vs. hold it? My club says always hold but tournament books disagree
Strategy • Posted by ClubPlayer_Opal • January 2002

At my local Scrabble club, the conventional wisdom is "never waste an S." But I've been reading the Edley/Williams "Everything Scrabble" and some of the tournament strategy PDFs I've found, and they suggest burning an S to score 8-10 extra points can be correct. My question is: is there a rule of thumb for when playing the S is correct? I'm a strong club player but only recently started attending rated tournaments. Rating around 1250.

Opal, "never waste an S" is club folk wisdom that's directionally correct but too absolute. Here is the tournament-standard framework:

The S is worth approximately +8 to +12 points of expected value in most rack contexts (it enables high-scoring plays later, scores extra points by pluralizing high-value words already on the board, etc.). The rule: burn the S when it gains you MORE than 8-12 points above your next-best play WITHOUT the S.

Practical test: compare your best play using the S vs. your best play without it. If the difference is more than ~10 points, burn the S - you've extracted its value. If the difference is less than 5 points, hold it for the large play it enables later. 5-10 points: depends on board state and turn number.

Jerome's framework is the standard one. I'd add a board-state dimension: an open board (lots of available spots to hook an S) increases the S's value. A closed board (few hook opportunities) decreases it. Endgame changes everything - in the last few turns, a held S that you can't play is worth zero, so the calculation shifts toward burning it earlier.

Double-S changes the calculation significantly. Two S tiles on a rack is a rack imbalance - you've concentrated 20+ points of expected value on two tiles, which reduces flexibility. With double-S, the threshold for burning one drops to about +5 points advantage. You're holding one insurance S and should actively find opportunities to play the second one. Holding two S tiles "because they might be useful" is a common intermediate-level mistake.

Related strategic principle: the blank is worth approximately 25-30 expected value points (three times an S). If you're holding both an S and a blank, burn the S almost always and protect the blank for a bingo setup. Rack balance also matters - an S paired with three or four vowels dramatically improves your ability to play your rack versus an all-consonant configuration. The S "fixes" a bad rack as well as scoring points.
