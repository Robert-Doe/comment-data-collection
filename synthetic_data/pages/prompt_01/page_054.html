CDC elk hair caddis sinking after 4-5 presentations - floatant not helping
Dry Fly Technique • Posted by WaderWilson • July 2002

I've been tying CDC (cul de canard) elk hair caddis patterns for the first time, using CDC fibers for the wing. They float beautifully on the first 3-4 casts, then start riding lower and lower until they're nearly submerged. I'm applying Gink floatant before fishing. I've seen people mention that regular floatant ruins CDC - is that my problem? What's the correct way to maintain a CDC fly? Fishing freestone streams in Colorado.

Wilson, you've diagnosed your own problem. Gink and most oil-based floatants are the worst thing you can apply to CDC. CDC fiber has natural oils that trap air between the barbs, giving it its remarkable buoyancy. Oil-based floatants clump and mat the fibers together, destroying the air-trapping structure.

The CDC rule: never apply oil floatant to CDC wings. Instead:
- Use a CDC-compatible powder like Frog's Fanny or Tiemco CDC & Elk
- After the fly sinks, don't re-apply floatant - instead dry the fly first
- The correct revival technique is below

The revival method for a waterlogged CDC fly: between casts, false cast the fly 4-5 times in the air. The whipping action flings out the water. Then apply a small pinch of dessicant powder (amadou or a commercial powder), rub gently between fingers, shake off excess. The fly will float as if new. This works 8-10 times before the fly truly needs replacing.

Amadou (a natural fungal material) is the traditional CDC drying material. Many fly shops carry it as a small patch. Worth having in your vest pocket.

Exactly right. Pre-treat CDC wing with powder floatant only; use Gink on the body/hackle if you want (though for a CDC pattern I'd skip it entirely and go powder-only). The elk hair in the wing also benefits from powder rather than Gink - it's a light pattern and needs the air-fiber structure intact. You'll find CDC flies float better than almost anything when treated correctly.

Also: if your CDC came off a cheap feather, the natural oils may have been stripped during processing. Quality CDC from Swiss or French suppliers (Marc Petitjean sells excellent material) has noticeably better floating characteristics. The fiber structure and oil content of the feather matters significantly. Good CDC is worth the premium for how it performs on the water.
