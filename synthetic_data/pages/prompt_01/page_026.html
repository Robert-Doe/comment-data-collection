First hive inspection ever - confused by what I'm seeing
Beginner's Corner • Posted by HoneyHopeful_Gail • May 2001

Just installed my first package of bees 2 weeks ago (3lb package, Italian queen). Did my first inspection today. I found the queen (she's marked yellow), saw eggs, and saw capped brood. But I also saw some cells with a white fluid/jelly in them, some pollen cells, and some cells that look like they have honey but aren't capped yet. How do I know if the hive is "doing well"? I didn't see any mites or obvious problems but I'm not sure what I should be worried about.

ApiaryMaster_Cal  — 40yr beekeeper — Posts: 5,678 — May 14, 2001
Gail, you've done everything right and your hive sounds healthy! Let me explain what you're seeing: The white jelly in cells is ROYAL JELLY - worker larvae are fed this for the first three days. This means your young larvae are being fed correctly. Pollen cells are the bees' protein store. Uncapped nectar that's being dehydrated will eventually be capped as honey. Seeing eggs + young larvae + capped brood is the Holy Trinity of a healthy hive. Your queen is laying well. Excellent first inspection!
BackyardBees_Martha  — Posts: 1,204 — May 14, 2001
To add to Cal's excellent answer: when looking for the queen, if you always see eggs you always know the queen was there within the last 3 days (eggs hatch in ~3 days). So you don't HAVE to spot the queen herself every time - spotting eggs is enough. Great that your queen is marked - yellow is 2001, which makes her easy to track year to year.

ApiaryMaster_Cal  — 40yr beekeeper — Posts: 5,678 — May 15, 2001
The color cycle: White (years ending in 1 or 6), Yellow (2 or 7), Red (3 or 8), Green (4 or 9), Blue (5 or 0). Mnemonic: "What Year Runs Green Blue" or "Will You Raise Good Bees." The sunken/discolored capped cells are worth watching. A few isolated ones may be normal (the bee inside died naturally). But if you see many sunken caps, or caps that smell foul/sour, or caps with a hole punched through them, that could indicate American or European Foulbrood. Check again next week and report back.
Varroa_Vigilant_Tom  — Posts: 2,301 — May 16, 2001
Also Gail - you mentioned not seeing any mites but I'd recommend a proper mite count rather than a visual inspection, which is very unreliable. A 24-hour sticky board count or an ether roll/alcohol wash will give you actual numbers. Even a new package can carry Varroa. I'd do a sticky board check now and compare at 30, 60, 90 days.

Varroa_Vigilant_Tom  — Posts: 2,301 — May 22, 2001
6 mites/day on a sticky board in May is borderline low. Many beekeepers treat at 50-100/day in summer. You're safe for now but monitor monthly. By late summer (Aug-Sep) mite loads increase rapidly as brood reduces heading into winter prep. Plan a treatment decision by August at the latest.
