Developed HP5 in Rodinal 1+50 - massive grain and low contrast - is my negatives ruined?
Film Development • Posted by NewDarkroom_Felix • October 2002

Just started developing my own film. Shot Ilford HP5 at box speed 400 ISO in a Canon AE-1 (outdoor portraits, overcast light). Developed in Rodinal 1+50, 20°C, 13 minutes with 3 inversions every 30 seconds. Negatives look very thin and flat - barely any visible shadow detail when held up to light. Contact prints are washed out with enormous visible grain and no contrast. I followed the Rodinal/HP5 time from a chart I found online. Is this batch of film ruined or did I make an error?

Felix, thin and flat negatives point to underdevelopment. Let's diagnose:

1. Development time: 13 minutes at 1+50 is on the shorter end for HP5. The Massive Dev Chart gives 11-13 minutes but some HP5 batches develop more slowly. I would start at 13 and if results are thin, try 15-16 minutes.

2. Agitation: 3 inversions every 30 seconds is LOW. Standard is 4-5 inversions every 30 seconds (or continuous for first 30 seconds, then 4 inversions every 30 sec thereafter). Insufficient agitation = underdevelopment = thin, flat negatives with blocked shadows.

3. Temperature: Are you certain of 20°C? A few degrees cold significantly slows development. Even 18°C would require a time increase of ~15-20%.

Adding to Victor: Rodinal 1+50 is known for pronounced grain because it's an acutance developer - it enhances edge sharpness at the cost of grain smoothness. HP5 is a medium-grain film but Rodinal brings out every grain structure. If you want finer grain with HP5, the classic choice is Ilford ID-11 or Kodak D-76. Rodinal works better with finer-grain films like FP4 where the grain at 1+50 is acceptable. Rodinal at 1+100 dilution with HP5 gives even more grain but gorgeous "stand development" results - but that's a more advanced technique.

The negatives are not entirely lost! Thin negatives can be printed on harder paper grades (grade 4 or 5 on variable contrast paper) to recover contrast. It won't fix the flatness completely but you may get usable prints. Alternatively, scan them at high resolution with a flatbed scanner (Epson V700 or similar), then boost contrast and levels in software - digital post-processing is more forgiving of thin negs than darkroom printing.

Your diagnostic instinct was correct: low agitation was the primary cause. The next roll will be much better with proper agitation (4-5 inversions every 30 seconds).

On the grain question: Rodinal + HP5 at 400 is a known combination for photographers who WANT large grain (street photography, reportage style). If you're shooting outdoor portraits and want smooth gradation, Anya's suggestion of ID-11 or D-76 is right. Those developers give HP5 creamy tones with minimal grain at 400 and work beautifully for portraits. Alternatively, rate HP5 at 200 in Rodinal for richer shadows and finer apparent grain.

Get a calibrated lab thermometer - the cheap plastic darkroom ones can be off by 2-3 degrees. A 2-degree error at 20C is a 10-15% development time difference. Inconsistent temperature control is responsible for more development frustration than any other single factor. Darkroom technique is 40% temperature management.
