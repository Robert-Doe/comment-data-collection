Digging by hand - 22 feet deep, soil changed to wet gravel - is this the water table?
Dug Wells • Posted by RuralHomestead_Walt • April 2001

Digging a hand-dug well on our 10-acre property in rural Kentucky. At 22 feet the soil changed from clay to wet gravel and fine sand. Water is seeping in slowly - about 2 inches per hour. I stopped digging for safety concerns. Is this the water table or just a perched water table? How do I determine if this is a usable aquifer vs. a temporary seep? We're planning to line the well with concrete tile rings. Should I dig deeper first?

WellDigger_Earl — 40 years well experience — Posts: 5,114 — Apr 12, 2001
Walt, wet gravel and sand at 22 feet with seeping water is an excellent sign. Gravel/sand saturated layers are classic unconfined aquifer material - this is likely a productive water table, not just a perched seep.

How to tell the difference: A perched water table sits on an impermeable layer above the main water table and tends to be thin. Let the water accumulate for 24-48 hours without pumping and measure the rise rate. If it continues rising steadily, you're in a connected aquifer. If it rises quickly then stops and holds at a fixed level, it may be perched.

Should you dig deeper: For a hand-dug well in Kentucky, 22 feet into a gravel/sand layer is potentially sufficient if the yield is adequate. Measure how much water recovers per hour. For household use you need at least 1-2 gallons per minute sustained, so about 60-120 gallons per hour recovery.
GroundwaterGuy_Phil — Hydrogeologist — Posts: 3,882 — Apr 12, 2001
The geology in rural Kentucky varies significantly by county. Your area may have limestone karst underlayers (central KY) or sandstone/shale formations (eastern KY). Both can support productive wells at your depth. The clay layer above your current seep is a good cap - it protected the gravel aquifer from surface contamination.

Critical: before any use, this water MUST be tested for coliform bacteria, nitrates, and local contaminants. County health departments often do free or low-cost well water testing. New wells also need to be "developed" (pumped vigorously to clear fine particles) and disinfected with chlorine before entering service.

WellDigger_Earl — Posts: 5,114 — Apr 13, 2001
1 gallon per hour sustained is on the low end but workable if you use a storage tank approach: a 250-500 gallon cistern fed by the well overnight gives you daytime supply buffer. The well refills continuously; you draw from the cistern during peak use. This is standard practice for low-yield hand-dug wells. I'd also recommend digging another 2-3 feet if you can do it safely - you may find the gravel layer deepens and yield improves significantly. Going 2 feet deeper sometimes doubles or triples recovery.
ConcreteRings_Jim — Posts: 2,334 — Apr 14, 2001
On the concrete tile lining: precast concrete well rings are the right choice. Line from the bottom up, setting rings as you dig the bottom few feet (the ring method where you dig under the ring and let it sink by gravity). Leave open joints in the bottom 3-4 rings to allow water entry - don't mortar the joints at the aquifer level. Mortar only from about 3 feet below ground surface upward to prevent surface water infiltration.
Anonymous — Guest — Apr 15, 2001
Safety note: working alone in a hand-dug well at 22 feet is genuinely dangerous. Soil collapse risk, oxygen depletion (check CO2 with a candle or O2 monitor before each entry), and gas pockets are real hazards. Always have someone at surface with a rope and communication. The Kentucky DNR has a hand-dug well safety guide free on their website.
