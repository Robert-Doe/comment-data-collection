Eyepiece upgrade for Jupiter/Saturn on an 8" f/6 Dobsonian - advice?
Equipment • Eyepieces & Accessories • Posted by NightOwl_Geoff • August 2002

I have an Orion SkyQuest XT8 (8" f/6, 1200mm focal length). Currently using the supplied 25mm Sirius Plossl (48x) and a 10mm Sirius Plossl (120x) for planets. At 120x Jupiter shows some detail but I want more. Budget around £150 for one or two eyepieces. At what magnification does the 8" Dobsonian start to lose resolution to atmospheric seeing? And which eyepiece manufacturers/models are worth the money for high magnification planetary work?

Geoff - the "theoretical" limit for an 8" is about 400x (50x per inch of aperture) but the PRACTICAL limit in UK seeing conditions is usually 200-250x on good nights, 150x on average nights. Don't chase maximum magnification - chase maximum quality at moderate magnification.

For planetary on an 8" f/6: a 6mm Baader Planetarium Ortho (200x) is a superb choice at £45. Orthoscopic eyepieces are unmatched for planetary sharpness - no edge correction needed because you're looking at a small bright object in the center of the field. Simple 4-element design = maximum transmission.

Seconding the Baader Ortho recommendation. I'd add: also consider a Barlow lens. A quality 2x Barlow (Televue Barlow is worth the money) doubles your effective focal lengths - so your 10mm Sirius at 120x becomes 240x. Two eyepieces + Barlow gives you far more magnification range than three or four individual eyepieces. Don't underestimate Barlows.

The Televue Powermate is optically superior to most Barlows because it telecentric design doesn't alter eye relief, doesn't darken the image measurably, and introduces essentially zero distortion. At f/6 it will slightly compromise the very edge of ultra-wide eyepieces but on a Plossl or Ortho you'll see no difference. It's an excellent optical accessory. Your math is slightly off though: 25mm + Powermate = 100x (not 50x x 2 at 1200mm focal length), and 10mm + Powermate = 240x. Still excellent coverage.

On the seeing question: if you haven't already, download the Clear Outside or Metcheck apps and start tracking the "seeing" forecast (Antoniadi scale). UK seeing is famously variable but the north and Scotland get better summer seeing than the south. For Saturn: the Cassini Division becomes a "reliable" view at around 150-180x on an 8" in good seeing.
