Spoon carving - hook knife tears fibers on end grain at bowl transition - how to approach it?
Spoon Carving • Posted by ChipCarver_Helen • February 2002

Carving my first spoon from a green birch branch, about 1.5 inches diameter. Using a Mora 162 hook knife for the bowl and a Mora 120 carving knife for the handle. Straight-grain sections carve beautifully but where the bowl meets the transition to end grain, the hook knife tears rather than slices - pulling up fibers instead of cutting cleanly. I'm using a push cut in the direction I think is "with the grain" but it doesn't seem to matter. Am I approaching the grain angle wrong?

Helen, this is the central challenge of spoon bowl carving. In a round bowl, the grain direction changes as you go around - there is NO single "with the grain" direction that works for the whole circumference.

The rule: always cut from the RIM DOWN toward the CENTER of the bowl, never from center outward. As you rotate around the bowl, your cut direction rotates too. Think of the bowl like a clock: at 12 o'clock, cut toward 6. At 3 o'clock, cut toward 6. At 9 o'clock, cut toward 6. Always toward center. This is "downhill" cutting that goes with the grain regardless of where you are in the bowl's circumference.

Adding to Lars: the Mora 162 is a good knife but the hook radius is quite tight, better suited to deep bowls than the shallow transition area you're describing. For the bowl transition where you're hitting end grain, try a lighter, shaving cut with the hook near flat against the wood rather than pulling through deeply. Skim cuts removing thin shavings are more forgiving of grain direction transitions than deep aggressive cuts.

Fresh birch is excellent for beginners - it's one of the traditional spoon woods of Scandinavia for good reason. The green (wet) state carves almost like soap with sharp tools. It does have occasional wild grain that can catch you out, but overall it's forgiving. Basswood (linden) is often recommended as the easiest, but I'd say green birch is equally learner-friendly. Your choice is fine.

Sharp tool note: the Mora 162 and 120 are sharp out of the box but not sharp enough for fine grain work. Before each session, strop both knives 20 times per side on a leather strop with green compound. You should be able to shave arm hair effortlessly. End grain is especially punishing of dull edges - the fibers compress and spring back rather than parting cleanly. Sharpness is about 80% of technique at the beginner stage.
