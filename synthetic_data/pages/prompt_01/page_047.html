Mortise and tenon racking under load - glue-up won't stay square
Joinery • Posted by NewJoiner_Dave • October 2002

Building a dining chair in white oak, hand-cut mortise and tenon joints. The joints fit well when dry - snug, no slop. But when I glue up the rear legs and the back rail, the assembly racks (parallelograms rather than staying square). I'm clamping with 3/4" pipe clamps. I've checked that my mortises are square to the face. The chair legs are tapered. Is the racking because of the angled joints or am I doing something wrong in clamping?

Dave, chair joinery is notoriously difficult because of the compound angles - your back legs are almost certainly not perpendicular to the rail in either plane. Racking during glue-up is almost always a clamping angle problem: if your clamp pads are not perpendicular to the rail centerline, the clamp pressure pushes the joint diagonally rather than straight in.

Solution: make angled clamping cauls that sit flush against the leg taper so your pipe clamps apply truly perpendicular pressure. Also: dry-clamp everything first, measure diagonal dimensions, and note which direction it racks. Then during wet glue-up you can apply a slight correcting pressure before the glue sets.

Adding to Rob: the diagonal measurement method is essential. Measure corner-to-corner both ways. Equal diagonals = square. If one is longer, clamp from that corner to the opposite corner (pulling the longer diagonal shorter). A long scrap of wood across the assembly with a clamp on it works perfectly for this correction.

Easiest method: hold a scrap block against the tapered face of the leg. The gap at one end is your wedge angle. Mark it, cut it on the bandsaw. Doesn't need to be mathematically perfect - just close enough that the clamp pad sits flush. You can also use hockey-puck rubber pads which conform somewhat to the taper. Many chairmakers use those for simplicity.

White oak is also highly temperamental with glue. Are you using PVA or hide glue? Hide glue is strongly preferred for chair joinery because its longer open time allows adjustment, and the slight shrinkage on cure actually tightens the joint. PVA can slide and cause racking. Also: white oak's open grain should be sized with diluted glue before final glue-up if tenons feel slightly loose after a dry fit.

Chairmaking tip I learned from a Windsor chair workshop: glue up in sub-assemblies with drying time between. Don't try to glue a whole chair at once. Rear legs + rear rail first (perfectly square), let cure 24 hours, then add seat rails. Each stage has one fewer variable to control.
