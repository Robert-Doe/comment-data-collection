Forum: Hand Tools • Topic started by MiterKing

I've been using a combination of waterstones (1000/4000/8000 grit) and a leather strop. Getting good results but wondering if anyone has tried the scary sharp method on sandpaper and whether it's actually as good as they claim. Discuss!

Scary sharp works absolutely fine and is cheaper to set up. Use float glass as your flat surface, stick the sandpaper down with water (it stays put and removes cleanly). Start at 120 grit if the back is rough, work through 220, 320, 400, 600, 1000, 1500, 2000. Finish on the strop. Edge is indistinguishable from my Norton waterstones honestly. Buy the glass at a hardware store cut to 12x18 inches for a few dollars.

I've used both extensively. Scary sharp is faster to get to a serviceable edge but the waterstones give me a better final result on my Japanese chisels. The slurry from the stones does something the sandpaper can't quite replicate. For Western bench chisels though the difference is minimal. Whatever method gets you sharpening more frequently is the best method.

MiterKing - I use a Veritas MkII honing guide for all my sharpening, scary sharp or stones. Freehand is fine once you have the muscle memory but a guide gives consistent results every time. For a beginner or intermediate it's worth every penny. Set the projection with their angle registration jig, lock it, and you're done. 25 degrees primary, 30 degrees micro-bevel on the final strop or stone.
