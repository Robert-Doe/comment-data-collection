Lead came joints turning chalky white after finishing - how do I get the dark patina back?
Lead Came Technique • Posted by GlassCutter_Nina • September 2001

I just completed my first lead came panel - a 16"x20" decorative piece with traditional patterns. The soldering went well and I used putty/whiting to seal the came. But after cleaning off the whiting, the solder joints and came have turned a dull chalky white/grey instead of the nice dark grey I've seen on finished panels. My instructor's panels have beautiful dark uniform patina. I used Novacan patina black solution on the joints only. What am I doing wrong?

Nina, the chalky white is caused by patina applied to a surface that wasn't clean enough. Flux residue or whiting residue on the solder surface blocks the patina chemical reaction and creates a whitish oxide layer instead of the intended dark patina.

The correct sequence for lead came finishing:
1) After soldering: clean all flux with a dedicated flux remover or isopropyl alcohol on a stiff brush. Flux residue must be removed or it will interfere with patina.
2) Apply putty/whiting compound and work in thoroughly. Remove with a stiff brush.
3) Then clean again with a damp cloth to remove all whiting dust.
4) Apply patina to DRY, CLEAN surfaces only. The came and joints must be fully dry.
5) Let patina react for 30-60 seconds, then buff off with steel wool (0000 grade).

Applying Novacan to a surface with whiting contamination gives exactly your result.

Also: Novacan patina black works best when applied with a soft cloth, not a brush. A brush can push the patina around without giving it enough contact time. Apply a small amount to a soft cotton cloth, rub it onto the came with circular pressure, and let it sit for the full minute before buffing. The chemical darkening needs dwell time on clean metal.

LeadMaster_Brigitte
Posts: 8,114 		Sep 23, 2001
Not permanently damaged at all. Lead and solder are quite forgiving. You can strip and redo:

1) Use fine steel wool (0000) and scrub all the came and joints vigorously to remove the chalky oxide layer. You want to see bright/clean metal again.
2) Wash the whole panel with dish soap and warm water, rinse thoroughly.
3) Allow to dry completely (I leave panels overnight).
4) Apply flux remover or isopropyl alcohol one more time for good measure.
5) Apply patina fresh on the clean, dry surface.

The panel will look as if you never applied patina the first time, then the second application will give you the dark, uniform result you want.

One addition: after successful patina, seal it with carnauba wax or a dedicated lead came wax product (Sculpt-Nouveau makes one). The wax protects the patina from re-oxidizing and gives the panel that deep, uniform dark sheen you see on quality finished pieces. Also protects the glass surface and the came from handling. Apply with a soft cloth and buff off. It's the difference between "finished" and "professional."

A note on Novacan specifically: it contains lead acetate and should be handled with gloves and used in ventilated conditions. The chemical is the same principle used in some patination processes - it deposits a black lead sulfide layer. Proper gloves and ventilation are not optional. Most experienced glass artists have a dedicated "patina cloth" that never touches anything else.
