Tuned to A440 but C#4 and F#3 sound sharp against everything else - what's happening?
Piano Tuning • Posted by HomePlayer_Dennis • February 2002

I own a 1960s Yamaha U1 upright that I've been tuning myself with a Peterson strobe tuner and the RPT unisons method. After tuning, A4=440 checks out perfectly across all three strings. But when I play chords, C#4 and F#3 seem noticeably sharp. I've re-tuned those notes multiple times and the tuner says they're correct. Is this my ears or is there an inharmonicity issue? I've been trying to tune for 8 months and this problem has persisted.

Dennis, what you're experiencing is inharmonicity - and you're not imagining it.

What's happening: A piano string's partials are not exact multiples of the fundamental - they're slightly sharp due to string stiffness (inharmonicity). A strobe tuner measures the fundamental frequency. When you tune C#4 to "correct" 440-derived pitch on the fundamental, the higher partials of that note are slightly sharp - and those partials are what you hear against neighboring chord tones.

Experienced piano tuners apply "stretch tuning" - the treble is tuned slightly sharp and the bass slightly flat of equal temperament to compensate for inharmonicity. A piano tuned perfectly to a strobe tuner will often sound sharp in the high treble when played against mid-register notes.

Margaret is right about inharmonicity. The Yamaha U1 is an excellent instrument but it has typical upright inharmonicity characteristics. The Peterson strobe is a superb tuner but it's designed to measure pitch, not to account for inharmonicity automatically.

Consider: the PTG (Piano Technicians Guild) stretch templates or Reyburn CyberTuner's inharmonicity measurement mode. CyberTuner can sample each string's inharmonicity coefficient and produce a custom stretch curve for your specific piano. It's not cheap (~$600) but it changes everything for a self-tuner.

Exactly correct. Professional piano tuning involves "tuning by ear" to optimize the sound of intervals, specifically octaves and fifths. A well-trained technician tunes the octave so the second partial of the lower note matches the fundamental of the upper note - which produces a slightly stretched octave compared to mathematically pure 2:1.

The Peterson strobe, used with a fixed 12-TET offset table, can approximate this - but the "right" stretch varies piano by piano based on scale design, string length, and string diameter. That's why CyberTuner's individual inharmonicity sampling is revolutionary for self-tuners.

One practical shortcut: the Peterson has "sweetened temperament" presets and a "stretch" adjustment. Try applying a mild stretch (the manual calls it "stretch compensation") - something like +0.2 cents/octave starting from middle octave. This won't be as precise as measured inharmonicity but may significantly improve the problem you're experiencing without the cost of CyberTuner.
