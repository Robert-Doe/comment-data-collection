Case-bound book spine cracking and pulling away from boards on first opening - glue issue?
Case Binding • Posted by NewBinder_Agatha • January 2002

I completed my first proper case binding (hardcover) using PVA glue throughout. The text block sewing is good (I think), I made the boards from 2mm book board, and used a bookcloth cover. But when I open the finished book, the spine lining (mull/super fabric) is cracking and I can see the spine slowly separating from the boards at the hinges. Within 10 openings the spine area looks damaged. I used craft store Elmer's PVA because I couldn't find bookbinding PVA. Is the glue the issue or am I missing something structural?

Agatha, you've identified both problems actually - glue and structure. Let me address each:

Glue: Elmer's craft PVA is too stiff when dry. Bookbinding PVA (Jade 403, Lineco, or similar) is formulated to remain slightly flexible when cured. This flexibility is essential at the hinges - the joint must flex thousands of times. Craft PVA dries brittle and cracks exactly as you describe. This is your primary problem.

Structure (hinge): The spine needs a cloth hinge strip (super/mull) that extends generously onto both boards (at least 3/4 inch). This distributes the opening stress. If your super only covered the spine without enough overlap onto the boards, there's nothing to anchor the flex point.

Adding to Ilse: the PVA issue is significant. Book-grade PVA typically has a plasticizer added that keeps it flexible after curing. You can also use a mixture: Jade 403 (or similar bookbinding PVA) diluted slightly with methyl cellulose creates an even more forgiving adhesive. This "PVA-MC mix" is what many professional binders use for pasting-in text blocks and covering boards - it gives more working time and the dried result is supple.

For mull/super: woven open-mesh fabric, not a solid material. The weave allows glue to penetrate both directions.

With 1/4 inch super overlap, the book is structurally compromised. You can apply a Japanese tissue and paste repair to the hinge area to stabilize it for light use, but a proper repair would involve pulling the case, strengthening the super, and recasing. For a first book, I'd recommend keeping this one as a learning reference and binding fresh. The lessons from a failed binding are very sticky in the memory. Your second binding will be dramatically better.
