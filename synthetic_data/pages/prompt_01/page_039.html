Disclaimer: this is general information, not legal/tax advice. Consult your CPA/attorney.

The backdoor Roth is legal. The IRS published guidance confirming this in Notice 2014-54 (though the strategy existed before that). The step-transaction doctrine is sometimes raised as a concern but the IRS has not challenged properly executed backdoor Roths.

Basic steps: (1) Make a non-deductible contribution to a Traditional IRA ($3,000 for 2002) (2) File Form 8606 with your tax return to establish the basis (3) Convert the Traditional IRA balance to Roth IRA (4) Pay tax only on any earnings between contribution and conversion (if done quickly, this is minimal)

The piece Mark is worried about (existing traditional IRA balances) is critical. The "pro-rata rule" applies when you convert. If you have any pre-tax IRA balances (SEP-IRA, SIMPLE IRA, or deductible Traditional IRA), the IRS treats all your traditional IRA money as ONE pool and applies the converted percentage to pre-tax and post-tax proportionally.

Example: you have $57,000 pre-tax in an old rollover IRA and add $3,000 non-deductible. Your pool is $60,000 = 5% post-tax. When you convert $3,000, only 5% is tax-free = $150 tax-free, $2,850 taxable. Effectively kills the strategy.

If your 401(k) doesn't accept rollovers, the workaround is to push your HR to change that policy - some plans can be amended. Alternatively: if you're self-employed on the side, a Solo 401(k) accepts IRA rollovers. Or wait for a future employer who does accept rollovers, then roll the pre-tax IRA into the new 401(k), THEN do the backdoor Roth. Some people hold the strategy in reserve for that reason. Your wife may not have pre-tax IRAs - has she checked? She can do a backdoor Roth immediately if her IRA slate is clean.

Lawrence is exactly right. And one more point: file Form 8606 every single year you make a non-deductible contribution, even if you don't convert that year. Basis established on Form 8606 is permanent and follows you. If you lose those records 20 years from now, you'll owe tax on money you already paid tax on. Keep Form 8606 copies with your tax records permanently.

Practical tip: open the Roth IRA at the same brokerage where your non-deductible Traditional IRA is. The conversion is then an internal transfer (not a distribution and redeposit) and is reported to the IRS automatically. Fewer chances for error. Vanguard and Fidelity both handle this smoothly.
