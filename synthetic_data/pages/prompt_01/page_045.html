Pinholes in every screen no matter what I do - losing my mind
Emulsion & Exposure • Posted by GaragePrinter_Kyle • May 2002

Home screen printer, printing T-shirts as a small side business. Using Ulano QTX emulsion, 160 mesh screens. Every single screen I coat has pinholes - tiny dots of ink bleeding through where the emulsion has gaps. I'm coating in my basement darkroom under a red safelight. Two coats on the print side, one on the squeegee side. Exposing with a 500W halogen at 12 inches for 90 seconds. The pinholes appear after the first print. I've tried everything I can think of.

 Emulsion_Expert_Dave  Posts: 4,882 — Pro Printer May 12, 2002 
Kyle, pinholes almost always come from one of three sources:

1) Dust - Any dust particle on the mesh before coating creates a pinhole. Clean your screens with a degreaser (Murakami Screen Wash or Dawn dish soap with a scrub brush), rinse thoroughly, and dry in a DUST-FREE environment. Even a slight breeze in a dusty basement is fatal.

2) Coating technique - Coating too fast creates air bubbles. Coat at a slow, even speed (about 3 inches per second). Apply firm, consistent pressure the whole length.

3) Under-exposure - Under-exposed emulsion washes out in the image area and also creates pinholes in the non-image area if the emulsion isn't fully hardened. 500W halogen at 12" for 90 seconds sounds borderline short. I'd try 120-150 seconds.
 TshirtPro_Angie  Posts: 1,234 May 12, 2002 
The dust issue is huge and almost always overlooked by home printers. Even in a "clean" basement there are microscopic particles. A good trick: after coating, stand the screen vertically in a CLOSED dark cabinet (not open air) to dry. This eliminates airborne dust settling on the wet emulsion. A repurposed wardrobe works perfectly.

 Emulsion_Expert_Dave  Posts: 4,882 May 13, 2002 
Exposure testing: use a step wedge calculator. Coat one screen, cut it into 6 pieces, expose each for a different time (60, 90, 120, 150, 180, 210 seconds). Wash out, print one stroke, and see which time gives the best detail and no pinholes. The correct exposure will have sharp edges and no spontaneous breakdown. It's a 30-minute test that eliminates years of guesswork.
 MeshCount_Maria  Posts: 2,109 May 14, 2002 
160 mesh is very fine for a general purpose screen. For plastisol ink printing on T-shirts, 110-135 mesh is more typical. Finer mesh = thinner emulsion layer = more prone to pinholes. If you're printing solid graphics, try a 110 mesh screen and see if the problem persists. QTX on 110 mesh with proper exposure should be bulletproof.
 Anonymous  Guest May 15, 2002 
Also check your film positive - if your transparency has pinholes (clear spots in the dark areas) light bleeds through those spots and creates corresponding pinholes in your emulsion. Hold your film positive up to a light and check the opaque areas. They should be 100% opaque - if you see any light through them, print at 100% black density and double-burn the positive.
