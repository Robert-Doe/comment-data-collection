When to use -san, -sama, -kun, -chan - I keep getting these wrong
Japanese • Grammar • Posted by BeginnerInTokyo • June 2001

I'm an English speaker who's been studying Japanese for 6 months and living in Tokyo for 3 months (work posting). I understand that -san is like "Mr/Ms" but I don't understand when to use -sama vs -san (is -sama only for extremely formal situations?), when -kun is appropriate (I've used it wrong before with female colleagues - very embarrassing), and what age rules govern -chan. My Japanese colleagues are too polite to correct me. Could native or fluent speakers help?

I'll try to explain clearly.

-san: Safe default for almost all situations with adults you don't know very well. Gender-neutral. Use this when unsure.
-sama: Reserved for customers (clients are addressed as X-sama in business settings), very formal letters, and some traditional contexts. If you use -sama to a colleague it sounds almost sarcastic.
-kun: Used by seniors to address juniors (male), by teachers to students (both genders in some contexts), or between close male friends. A male colleague using -kun to a female colleague can feel patronizing - this is why your female colleague reacted.
-chan: Young children, close female friends, beloved pets. Using for adults unless very close = childish/condescending.

In Japanese business emails: -san is used even with close colleagues in written form because email is considered semi-formal. Only in very casual internal chat (instant messaging/IRC-style) might you see names dropped. This differs from verbal communication where very close colleagues might use just the given name (with or without -chan between women friends). If you want to observe: look at how your Japanese colleagues address each other in CC'd emails. That will tell you the specific culture of your company.

Adding the educational dimension: at school, teachers address all students with -san in modern Tokyo schools (gender neutral, equal treatment). Some older teachers still use -kun for boys, -chan for girls in elementary school. Textbooks use -san throughout. This is shifting toward -san as the universal student honorific as gender-neutral language becomes more important.

Don't forget -sensei (teacher/doctor/expert) - this is an honorific for specific roles, not a name suffix per se. Your doctor, lawyer, professor, and martial arts instructor are all -sensei in the appropriate context. Using -san for your doctor isn't wrong but -sensei is more respectful.
